The Science of Sneezing

Causes


Sneezes, or stemutation, are usually caused by nasal mucosa irritation by foreign particles; other causes include exposure to bright light, temperature change, cold or hot air, or viral infection, among others. Sneezing is also triggered by sinus nerve stimulation caused by nasal conestion and allergies.










Consequences


While generally harmless, sneezes of unhealthy inividuals spread disease through infectious aerosol droplets; these droplets commonly range from .5 to 5 micrometers. A sneeze can produce 40,000 droplets. A sneeze expels air with mucus containing foreign particles or irritants from the mouth and nose in an explosive, spasmodic involuntary action. The release of air and bioparticles from th enasal secretion can reach up to six feet. The resulting droplets can last on surfaces from two to eight hours. Viruses from sick individuals can access a respiratory system either directly from the droplets reaching the eyes, nose, or mouth, or when a person touches his or her face. The sound of sneezing also can disturb a quiet environment.






Shielding


In practice, when sneezing, people cover their mouths with their hands or turn in a different, more appropriate direction or place to avoid influencing others reducing transmission or contamination. Afterwards, people use tools such as napkins, handkerchiefs, santizers or soaps.

Devices such as face masks, eye goggles, or face shields or guards can be recommended and useful at times to limit influencing others or to protect from sneezing from others.









Prevention

Preventative measures include physical prevention such as holding one's breath and pinching the bridge of the nose; avoiding, minimizing, or eliminating irritants, pathogens, and allergens; and nasal strips. However, there is yet to be a device for the general public designed for anti-sneeze (preventative purposes for sneezing).
